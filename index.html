Learning Generalizable Hand-Object Tracking from Synthetic Demonstrations

Abstract

We present a framework for learning versatile dexterous manipulation directly from synthetic data, relaxing the traditional dependency on human demonstrations.

Our approach makes two key contributions: (1) a systematic decomposition of manipulation into foundational meta-skills, coupled with ManiSynth—an efficient pipeline generating diverse synthetic demonstrations from programmatically created grasp configurations; and (2) HOT—a Hand-Object Tracking imitation framework that bridges synthetic-to-physical transfer through reinforcement learning, delivering a generalizable controller conditioned on target hand-object states.

Our method demonstrates remarkable versatility across diverse object categories and hand morphologies without requiring any real-world demonstration data. Through extensive evaluations, we show that our approach enables dexterous hands to execute challenging, long-horizon tasks including dynamic throwing, reactive catching, and agile in-hand reorientation. These results represent a significant step toward scalable foundation controllers for manipulation that can learn entirely from synthetic data, breaking the demonstration bottleneck that has historically constrained progress in dexterous manipulation.

Method Overview

We propose a novel system for generalizable hand-object tracking purely from synthetic demonstrations. (1): We propose ManiSynth, a scalable framework that procedurally synthesizes meta-skill demonstrations of dexterous manipulations. (2): Leveraging these synthetic data, we train a Hand-Object Tracker (HOT) using reinforcement learning with unified HOI imitation reward. (3): During inference, HOT can track long-horizon, complex, and dynamic manipulation sequences.

ManiSynth Pipeline

Given a dexterous hand and an object, ManiSynth automatically synthesizes demonstrations for eight meta tasks of dexterous manipulations. The pipeline first generates diverse grasp configurations by optimizing force-closure conditions. These grasps configurations are then systematically transformed, stitched, or interpolated according to task-specific kinematic rules. Our method generalizes across different hand morphologies and object geometries, enabling full coverage of the manipulation space with scalability.
